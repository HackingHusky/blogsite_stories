But we are seeing a huge trend here. It's likely that companies are experimenting with AI but may not be budgeting correctly or seeing a return on investment. We often overlook that IT is a maintenance field, and it doesn't have an ROI unless you are an MSP (managed service provider) or you provide IT services directly, such as creating software and helping customers install it.

My background includes working at a few places, such as an MSP that involved direct access to someone’s server or physical infrastructure, such as Cougar Mountain Software in Idaho, a financial software company that provided customer support for installing SQL, and the product Denali. If the customer had issues installing, they could buy support minutes to help install. That's one example of ROI in our IT department.

However, most places don’t provide this support or aren't part of the business model. Equipment costs a lot, but it's our job in tech to help keep the car running, no different than a mechanic. So when a company expands, so do its needs for security, network, and assets. I could go on.

So, I can guess what is happening here. CEOs look at the investment, buy a certain number of tokens, don’t have revenue from the token, product, or service, and do not return their value to the company. Has to explain what happened to the shareholders. The CEO blames AI, which now can’t really pay its employees, so it has to make cuts. Lays off employees; still has this AI model for customers, but now can’t budget for security, its infrastructure, or the employees to handle it; also could be losing customers. Hey, another pattern is happening right before our very eyes.

And with that, the market is tough right now. It could be poor leadership decisions, or they really can’t see the ROI. Let's take pentesting as an example. Why wouldn’t we see an ROI for it? It is a service, but it's preventive maintenance. When a report is handed in, that company won’t see its ROI, but the car they have now can run much more smoothly. There is now a paper trail in the IT department, providing physical evidence to inform changes and equipment budgeting for the road ahead. But with AI, it can be a catch-22. No prompts are the same; they can produce allucinations. It can be a huge mess compared to seeing actual psychological evidence, like a report.

So what does that mean for the field? There’s going to be a need for help desk, network engineers, system administrators, SOC, etc. The needs and timing changes, like scanning for vulnerabilities, come faster. If AI becomes more capable of finding zero-day vulnerabilities and executing zero-day attacks, the need for experts will increase. We are in this weird bubble, and that bubble is on leadership, not the technology. Some may agree to disagree.

The way I see it, learning right now is way more important than ever. As it evolves, the need will increase, and so will the techs that go with it. Who knows, we may have written laws about AI, or we may get the cyberpunk dystopia Ray Bradbury wrote for us: “There Will Come Soft Rain.”
