Thinking about the Sec+?

I took this test last May, but never really got around to blogging about it. But if you want to start working in security and you're new to the field, you need this cert. Some may argue and ask why; however, this cert is DOD (Department of Defense) compliant. That’s why its great to have. Here's my cert:

Getting this cert was a pain, and my study habits weren't the best either, until I started going over the material. If you want to transition to cybersecurity, do you need the Net+? Now, if you want to land a help desk role, an A+ is excellent, but it's not really needed in the market. If you show or know how AD functions, how to onboard PCs, and the troubleshooting process, it's a waste of time. Unless you really don't know it, nor have had any education. Then get the A+, and study for the Net+, unless you want to be a network engineer; having both the Net+ and Sec+ is good. However, if you focus only on Cisco network switches, get the CCNA. If you want all three Certs, though, it's your journey; Professor Messor got you covered in test exams, studying, and almost everything.

Things I learned while taking it: give yourself about two months to study. Don't let the imposter syndrome take over, and you start to overthink this test. I most certainly did.

During the test, seriously skip the PBQs (performance-based questions). They don't exist until the end, and trust me, if you are good with multiple-choice questions, you can easily have enough time. You have about 90 minutes on the test; it took me close to 45 minutes before I did the PBQ's.

From the practice test site:

https://www.comptia.org/en-us/certifications/security/practice-questions/

A business development team reports that files are missing from the database system, and the server login screens display a lock icon that requires users to contact an email address to access the system and its data. Which of the following attacks is the company facing?

A. Rootkit

B. Ransomware

C. Spyware

D. Bloatware

Using process of elimination here, it cannot be A. When you install a rootkit, it is more likely to remain quiet. A red team engagement will do this. I have also done this before, like setting up a C2 server and gaining access to a machine. We haven't exfil yet; this is still the enumeration stage. That would be like a backdoor. Spyware is the same thing. And bloatware is something already installed from the OS. So it has to be B Ransome.

After the test, you be introduced to a survey, and after that, you’ll know when you pass. Regardless if your taking it online or on a testing campus. I will say it’s not really a beginner test, but it's extremely doable.

Now that you have this certification, are you qualified for the cybersecurity field? Sorta? Now, let’s say you've been studying other subjects like pentesting or SOC roles. You've gotten familiar with the tools. Do yourself a favor: make a YouTube video or a blog post about it, and put the instructions in GitHub or on your blog. Certs just don’t cut it, really; however, doing projects and asking yourself hard tech questions like “what is the difference between TCP and UDP?” This question has always come up in the interviews I've had. I will say the Sec+ does give you a leg up.

If you dive into a day in the life roles that you want to look at, you’ll need to study a bit more. I don’t know what it could be like for you. I stopped using roadmaps and went after the role I wanted. However, this is a great stepping stone to get if you are truly thinking about cybersecurity. But also do the extra mile: make a tool, build a home lab, find a business-impact issue, and make it a solution. Make mock reports. This also expands your portfolio.
