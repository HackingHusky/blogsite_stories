Guide to OCSP: Things I Learned Along the Way from Failure.

“One must make the warrior walk his everyday walk” — Miyamoto Musashi

Call it Sisyphus Syndrome, but I failed the OSCP twice last year. Next spring, I will retake it. My mentor saw it in my eyes; I took the failure very personally. I asked for some real, hands-on projects to do, but only a small bug bounty. However, after my previous failed attempt, I was introduced to a Red Team engagement. He wanted to see how much I really enjoyed it. We had official rules of engagement; however, to really sum it up for our rules, "Don't take anything out of production. Communicate with the White Team about your attacks. Everything else is a free game. 100% of this is black boxed. Don't get caught."

I only done some Penetration Tests, but never a Red team. For those who don't know, a Red Team is a simulated hacker group/insider threat that isn't trying to get caught. While the Blue Team responds to the attack, the SIEM detects it. The White Team serves as the Referee between the teams. The Blue Team is being tested as the Red Team does everything it can to breach the defenses. This goes beyond the OSCP, and honestly, after failing twice, I didn't know what to expect. My other mentor took me aside one day and said, "Just be smart about it."

I've been in IT for a while. So, thinking about this type of penetration test, I thought, "Active Directory, I know like the back of my hand." I kept it simple. I didn't need Kali Linux; I used a Windows 8 VM, a bit of Social Engineering, Nmap, and Rubeus. I was in the domain within about two hours. The next day, after I had finished submitting my report, I felt heavy, thinking, "Oh, I must have been caught." During our debrief, the Blue Team couldn't find any logs of my actions; after retesting after submitting the new SIEM rules, they fixed the issue. However, without that test, we wouldn't found a huge blind spot. This was a real-world test, not just a Capture the Flag (CTF) event.

That lit a fire in me, knowing not only that I can do the work, but also that I don't need to make this test complicated. I've been approaching Penetration Testing all wrong. Like the PJPT, that was real-world, and I was able to get that cert quickly. But now I could put it all together. It wasn't imposter syndrome; I was lacking resolve after failing. That was the lesson I really needed to learn.

The OSCP is brutal. For those that don't know, it's called the Offensive Security Certified Pentester. OSCP for short. You only get 24 hours to compromise 5–6 machines. 3 machines are Active Directory, and 2 to 3 standalone machines that you also need to compromise. They're scored differently, but you need a total of 70 points to pass the test and submit a report. The hard part is that you are extremely time-limited on the test. To get points, you need to CTF each machine. I had done about 40–50 machines as practice across many platforms, but nothing compared to what I learned after I failed twice.

“Fall seven times, rise eight.” — Japanese Proverb

So after Red Team engagement, I needed some RnR. I went on vacation to Japan. I actually always wanted to go to Japan, and I had a blast in Osaka. While wandering the city, I read a lot on the train. And, a lot of people in IT can super relate to this. We are all constantly reading manuals, as if we were repairing a 1993 Geo Metro. So the moments when I didn't have to read about AI pentesting, the OSCP, trying to fix issues in Azure, etc. I wanted to read and pick up the mindset of Ikiagi and Bushido. I was doing boxes and making notes, but did I have the discipline for the OSCP? No. That was mistake number 1. Regardless, part of the exercise I just did, I gave it my full attention. But for an exam, I didn't do it twice. Nor did it give it the respect it deserved. Not like I did for the Red Team exercise. This was mistake number one.

So, oddly (yes, using a Husky as an internet persona doesn't help this next part), I started reading No Longer Human by Osamu Dazai. Honestly questioning himself, societal norms, self-destruction behavior, and being "disqualified as a human being". It was raw, emotional, and oddly questioning my own Ikiagi. However, the three books were necessary for me. As I walked through the city after reading all three, I understood what I needed to question, and I really needed to dig deep. I found what I was lacking, what it takes to get to that next level, and am I doing everything within my power to really get into Offensive Security?

Sure, I created a YouTube channel, and I am still discovering what I could be. However, is it fitting for my own Ikiagi? Why do I want this path? I always been great with computers. I'll be personal: part of me was thinking, was this another checkbox I was hitting for my old man? After he passed, I lost much of my motivation. He was my rock, someone that not only looked up to, but also asked for advice. Losing that led me into a depression. After reading No Longer Human, I found I was in the same state of questioning why.

However, one company I worked for had a security incident. As a System Admin, I had to take control of the situation, and I had been with the company for barely a year. The company hosted AWS, and I was told that I had to find the issue without an IAM User account. So, with no SIEM tool and no access to CloudWatch, I had to conduct my own investigation. And the server was compromised by a ransomware attack. So I related to Dazai with my own career. However, I found the attacker's IP using AutoSpy, Wireshark, Event Logs on the Active Directory server, the compromised user, and a prayer. The attacker was still on the network, and I stopped them in their tracks when I saw activity after spinning up a backup image of the EC2 instance (Virtual Machine on AWS).

My coworker, since there were only two people in our department, looked at me and said, "Regardless of what happens in this situation, you have a talent for this. You should look into cybersecurity." I thought it was another day in the office, to tell you the truth.

That experience, too, led to Penetration Testing, because I wanted to be the one to find the vulnerabilities before an attack did. Then one mentor showed me the meaning of Ikiagi. It wasn't about whether I had talent; I just loved learning about Cyber. Whether it was Blue Team, Red Team, Purple Team (one that does both Blue and Red), application security, threat hunting, etc., I loved it, but there was one thing I needed to agree on. You can be passionate, but without discipline, you can set yourself up for failure.

I bring this up because it led me to study, and I wanted to take this test, but I was chasing a dead end. Learn from these mistakes that I made on this test. If you really want to pass, respect the tech and have self-discipline. You need to make this your daily walk. Otherwise, you will set yourself up for failure. So, being a bit of a weirdo, I started studying the mindset of Miyamoto Musashi, the world's deadliest samurai, while I was in Osaka. It all came together. I am not him; sure, there are better hackers, but I am me, and I carry his quotes into my work. In Bushido, the moral code emphasizes virtues such as loyalty, honor, respect, and courage, alongside rigorous physical and mental training, meditation, and a focus on duty, integrity, and self-mastery over emotion, culminating in a life of purpose and unwavering character, even in the face of death. Now not facing death here. However, I am facing challenges, like others before me and those on a similar path to mine. I didn’t. I was thinking it head-on, I view it as another test. Don’t do that, respect the process. This was mistake number one for me.

“Today is victory over yourself of yesterday; tomorrow is your victory over lesser men.” — Musashi

There was a lot of exposition dump, only because if you are thinking of this test, or if you have already failed. It may not be technical. The OSCP is not realistic. That is a fact: you are studying to find the flag, not actual pentesting. What you get really good at is manual enumeration and methodology. That is actually a huge takeaway. So think first, I am approaching this test as accurately as I can.

Also, this applies to anything from Hack the Box’s older machines, TryHackMe labs, Hack Smarter Labs, a great friend from Hack Academy, and more.

Don’t just watch or read a walkthrough, do the work of making your own, and see how much faster you get. Review the cyber kill chain as you work on a box; you’ll know where you're failing. This was mistake number 2 for me. Instead, I created a YouTube channel to combat this and also wrote a log post here on Medium.

Next, you have to train not only the mind but also the body. I’ll put this as optional for the most part, but the biggest thing, especially with my first mistake. Make sure you get sleep. At least 7–8 hours of sleep, unless you can live on 6 hours. This is critical.

Second, if you are cramming everything into a month, stop and reschedule the test; you have 120 days to order. If you are nervous, that’s one thing. If you scatter, all over the place, and not centered, don’t take the test. Your notes may be all over the place.

Also know when to rest. A box a day is excellent for 30 days, even if you struggle with one, but remember to enjoy your hobbies and downtime. To go over the rules of the OSCP, straight from the source:

OSCP+ Exam Guide
Please read this entire document carefully before beginning your exam! This article provides information on: Exam…help.offsec.com

You are given a live network VPN, with 23 hours and 45 minutes to complete the technical exam. After completing the exam, you have another 24 hours to submit a report. That link shows how to submit a report. I’ll break down what they want.

You have to show a step-by-step process of a “how to” once you pwn a box. So take notes in something like Notion, and add a “```” to include the commands, their results, and screenshots of the steps you took. Then your last snapshot should be the following commands:

  1. ipconfig/ifconfig
  2. whoami/id
  3. cat/root/proof.txt or local.txt or Windows type C:\Users\Administrator\Desktop\Proof.txt or local user path, local.txt

All flags are usually in the root/Admin folder, unless the user is different. You’ll know once you see the local.txt, that means you pwn half the box. If you don’t include that in the report, you could miss points and fail the test.

You have 3 stand-alone machines and an AD set of three machines. The points are different.

Restrictions:

No spoofing or posing, LLNMR posing will get you failed. You can use Responder, but it must be turned off. No commercial tools, that’s Burp Suite Pro—no automatic exploitation tools, which means no SQLmap. Because you can get a shell with SQLmap, so, for this test, it's not pointless to learn, but it's pointless to use. But you can use Burp to test for SQL injection. Example:

Using Burp to Exploit Blind SQL Injection Bugs
Using Burp to Exploit Blind SQL Injection Bugs. In the Using Burp to Detect Blind SQL Injection Bugs article, we…portswigger.net

You can use Burp (and will know how) to perform an SQL injection. This is allowed because you're not autopwning the service or getting a shell; this is website enumeration. The same goes for authentication, brute-force, or LFI/RFI attacks. Fair game. But you can’t use a tool that enumerates and gives you a shell. That will get you failed.

No mass scanners, meaning no Nessus, OpenVAS. You can use RustScan, Nmap, and more.

No AI chat bots. However, you can’t turn off Google AI overview. So you look something up on Google, that’s a search. That’s 100% safe. You can not use ChatGPT, Gemini, or Deepseek. They cannot tell you how to do something. That will be an automatic fail. But if you open Chrome to search for exploit xyz, that’s okay. It is a Google search.

Metasploit is restricted to one box. So if you have 60 points and need the last 10 and haven’t used them, it's okay. But it's only for the machine that you used it for. However, with a strong methodology, you probably don’t even need it. Remember, if there is a Metasploit for it, there is a GitHub repo for it.

OffSec’s website also has a template you can use for reporting. I would use that.

Tips for the exam: This section is for those actively studying for the test.

Low-hanging fruit. Train your Brain for these rules:

  1. When nmap is scanning, start looking for standard attack ports, 21, 80, 135/445, 139, and 88. 88, you know you have an AD server, use krubute. 21 ftp, do I have anonymous access? 80, run dirsearch and whatweb, what directories are open? 445 and 139 (smb/RPC ): Do I have anonymous access? Start thinking about where I have access, then go through the process of elimination about that port.
  2. For websites, look for the robots.txt files; someone may have made a misconfiguration. Take note of the technology. Start thinking, this website hosts a server, so if I were an upload function, where would I be stored? Do I have admin/admin access?
  3. This one is after the website. If you know it's a Linux machine, do you have console access on that website? If you do, you can likely get the local flag first. After that, gain shell access, and it's just a matter of privesc from there.
  4. Seriously, use manual enumeration on the machines you compromised. Do not use LinPEAS or WinPEAS first. Save time by doing this first:

Linux:

sudo -l: Do I have sudo privileges?

find / -user root -perm /4000 2>/dev/null: SUIDS

uname -a: Kernel version, could be dirty cow!

crontab -l: Cron jobs?

netstat -tulpn: Can I port forward?

Windows:

Whoami /priv- god potato if impersonation is open.

systeminfo

netsh wlan show profiles

use powerview/powerup

AD:

Get-NetGroup *admin*

Get-NetGroupMember -GroupName “Domain Admins”

5. If you get a user's credentials, use BloodHound first. Then, while it's running, use netexec for smb, RDP, WinRM, and LDAP. You could strike gold first. But that fails. Check Bloodhound; then you have a pwned user you can use to move laterally.

6. Hack tricks are great for web enumeration for LFI. Test everything about that website. Run dirsearch or gobuster with a different wordlist.

7. I recommend Blitz for time management:

8. Stick to OffSec’s reporting template. You’ll most likely never need it again.

9. Really know how to pivot with these two tools:

echo "AddType application/x-httpd-php .xxx" > .htaccess

Then use the repo:

11. ENUMERATE, ENUMERATE, ENUMERATE, ENUMERATE! Repeat that if you're stuck and take your time. If you don't click the box at first, come back to it.

12. I started with AD first before the boxes, which was correct. It takes about 3–4 hours. So, really nail down AD hacking as best as you can.

13. Take breaks, drink water, bring snacks, eat meals, you know the drill. But if you're 18 hours in and struggling to find the next flag, with not much progress. It’s ok to re-read and think of a better game plan. There’s something in your methodology.

14. Make reports/how-tos! You don’t have to share them, but this is an open-note test. If you see a box that resembles one from HackTheBox or TryHackMe, use it. It’s a fair game.

I also added a bit of my two cents here about the certification and its process. Now, do you need it in 2026? Unless it's asked for, probably not. Being honest. This test is not an accurate measure of skill, aside from time management and manual enumeration. Hack the Box Certified Penetration Testing Specialist is just as good. But it's a bit harder. Luckily, though, it's 10 days, not 24 hours.

However, realistically, given the noise I hear about certs, the value of learning something new and applying it to a new career feels challenging. However, let's say you go into a Pentesting interview: you bring in your mock reports and the Python tools you built, knowing what XSS means (did that intentionally), and how to translate technical knowledge into a business impact report. The purpose of a Pentest is to show what could happen and its impact on the company. You want to be the first to find it, not an attacker.

So why take this cert? This is something I wanted to do from the start of my journey. Not a day goes by without hearing about a new supply-chain attack or a misconfigured n8n website. “It’s not a bug, it's a feature,” mentally is really starting to break a lot more products than solutions. Sometimes, when building a product, you can end up with a messy, buggy one that, over time, can be fixed and improved. Losing its functionality for something shiny ruins the product. I wanted to be the one who saw it, acted first, and contributed to society's safety. Part of my Ikiagi, I love learning about this, love making tools, I love helping others be safe online, and love the thrill of finding an exploit first.

It's the thrill of, can I do this in less than 24 hours? That’s a challenge I wanted to take and say, “I took it and powned it.” Called it a rite of passage. You can take certs, but the value of skill is more critical, like building a YouTube channel, blog post, or mock reports. If you do a cert, do it for yourself to challenge yourself. That’s the most important lesson, really.

The cost of the OSCP is about $1700 for 90-day labs, a pdf, and how-to guides for 90 days. The tier one for a year is about $2k or more, with a learning plan included. Hack the Box is about $700 in training and $210 for the cert. Either way, for these power-house exams, you do go down a rabbit hole of learning, but if you overcome the hurdles, this test becomes easier. And in the field, it's nothing like the test. I am more excited to retake it because of the thrill of the challenge.

If you are taking it and AD is hard to get down, take the PJPT from TCM Security. That way, you get a solid foundation for AD pentesting.
